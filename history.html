Timeline

The film industry has existed for over 100 years. Here are some important "firsts" for women in this male dominated industry.

Silents

  • First woman to direct a full length feature: Lois Weber for The Merchant in Venice (1914).
  • First woman to go nude in a motion picture: Audrey Munson in The Inspiration (1915) playing an artist's model.

1920s

  • First woman to win an Oscar: Janet Gaynor won the award for Best Actress on May 16, 1929. After winning at age 22, she held the record for "youngest best actress winner" for almost six decades until Marlee Matlin won at age 21 for Children of a Lesser God in 1987.

1930s

  • First Oscar win for Katharine Hepburn: On March 16, 1934 Hepburn won for her role in Morning Glory. She would go on to win 3 more times. As such, she holds the distinction of winning the most Oscars for acting, male or female.
  • First woman to receive a "special" Oscar: Shirley Temple became the first woman and child star to be honored with a "special" Oscar when she received a miniature Juvenile Oscar in February 1935.
  • First woman to win Best Supporting Actress Oscar: Gale Sondergaard for her role in Anthony Adverse (1936).
  • First woman to win Back-to-Back Best Actress Oscar: Luise Rainer in The Good Earth and The Great Ziegfeld.

1940s

  • First Black woman to win an Oscar: Hattie McDaniel won Best Supporting Actress on February 29, 1940 for her role as "Mammy" in Gone with the Wind (1939). With this award she was also the first Black woman to be nominated for an Academy Award. McDaniel faced great discrimination during the Academy Awards ceremony after being seated at a segregated table in the back of the room. The discrimination continued after the ceremony when she was denied access to a "no-blacks" club her white co-stars were going to. Another Black woman would not win an Oscar for fifty years until Whoopi Goldberg won Best Supporting Actress for her role in Ghost (1990).
  • First actress to become an elected US official: Helen Gahagan known for her titular role in SHE (1935) served in the Congress for California beginning in 1945.
  • First winner of Cannes Best Actress: Michele Morgan for her role in La Symphonie Pastorale (1946).

1950s

  • First woman to win Best Actress for her debut performance: Shirley Booth (1952). She remains the only one of thirteen women to have won an Oscar for their debut performance.
  • First woman to receive an honorary regular-sized Oscar: Greta Garbo in 1954. Another woman would not receive one for fourteen more years.
  • First Black woman to be nominated for Best Actress: Dorothy Dandridge for the musical Carmen Jones (1954). The Best Actress award would not be won by a Black woman for almost fifty more years.
  • First pregnant Oscar winner: Eva Marie Saint in April 1955 for On the Waterfront (1954).
  • First Asian woman to win an acting Oscar: Miyoshi Umeki won Best Supporting Actress for her role in Sayonara (1957). She remains the only Asian woman to have ever won an Acting Oscar.
  • First women on the Hollywood Walk of Fame: In September 1958, Joanne Woodward, as well as two silent film actresses, Olive Borden and Louise Fazenda, received coveted stars on the Walk of Fame.

1960s

  • First woman to win the "Triple Crown" of acting: Ingrid Bergman was the first woman to complete the trio of an Emmy, Oscar, and Tony when she won her Emmy in 1960.
  • First woman to be paid $1 million for a single film: Elizabeth Taylor for Cleopatra (1963). Although the movie was considered a flop on a cost-to-gross ratio, she actually earned around $7 million due to different contractual pieces.

1970s

  • First woman to win the EGOT: Barbra Streisand completed this feat of an Emmy, Grammy, Oscar, and Tony by 1970.
  • First woman to win an Oscar and an Emmy in the same year: Liza Minnelli for Cabaret (1972) and Liza with a Z (1972).
  • First woman to win Best Picture at the Oscars: Julia Phillips for The Sting (1973).
  • First woman nominated for Best Director at the Oscars: Lina Wertmuller for Seven Beauties (1976). After producing this film, she continued to produce prominent films of the '70s.
  • First Meryl Streep Oscar nomination: The Deer Hunter (1978). She has received 21 nominations making her the record holder for most Academy Award nominations.

1980s

  • First actress on a US postage stamp: it is widely considered to be Ethel Barrymore who was portrayed on a stamp with two other members of her family in 1982.
  • First actress to create a fitness empire: Jane Fonda. Workout Starring Jane Fonda is still the best selling fitness video of all time. She was only 45 at the time of its release.
  • First back-to-back Best Actress Cannes Winner: Barbara Hershey for Shy People (1987) and A World Apart.
  • First woman to direct a blockbuster: Penny Marshall directed Big (1988).

1990s

  • First Black woman to direct a movie that won general theatrical release: Julie Dash with Daughters of the Dust (1973).

2000s

  • First Black woman to win Best Actress: Halle Berry for her role in Monster's Ball on March 24, 2002. She remains the only Black woman to have won Best Actress
  • First American woman to be nominated for Best Director: Sofia Coppola for Lost in Translation (2003). Her fellow American, Kathryn Bigelow, would make history by becoming the first woman to win this award seven years later.
  • First transgender actress to play a recurring trans character on a network tv show: Candis Cayne for her role in Dirty Sexy Money (2007).

2010s

  • First woman to win Best Director: Katheryn Bigelow won for The Hurt Locker on March 7, 2010. She is still the only female director to have been honored with this award
  • First woman director to have a film gross over $1 billion: Jennifer Lee for Frozen (2013).
  • First openly trans woman to be nominated for an Emmy: Laverne Cox for her role in Orange is the New Black in 2013.
  • First Indigenous woman to be nominated for Best Actress: Yalitza Aparicio for her work in Netflix-produced Roma (2019).

Future

Looking back over the past century of film, it is easily seen how much progress has been made in showcasing the female voice in film. However, there are still many more barriers to breakthrough and many more milestones to reach. In short, there is still much more progress to be made. It is not only enough to showcase some female's voices, but a diverse set of female voices.
